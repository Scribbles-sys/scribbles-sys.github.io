The Matchmaker & Raffishness

Return to catalog

Jesus was made lowly. He was born into an animal trough, was an unassuming carpenter, died the death of a traitor, et cetera. It is easy in our modern era to abstract this concept into something silly. There is a standard so high that it is unattainable to us that the Christ held Himself to during His life on Earth and that He holds to this day. There were a lot of things Jesus did not do and called his followers not to do. He never launched substanceless attacks on others, He never approved of anyone's sin regardless of how small, and He never suggested that one should never appeal to authority, holy or godless the authority may be, to give a small sample. There is a true and right place for undercutting only because the society we live in is godless. The Lord's foolishness is in truth wisdom and holiness. [Job 12; Psalm 75; Proverbs 1; 1 Corinthians 1:18-31, 3:10-23, 4:6-13; James 3:5-18] The characters in The Matchmaker present a youthful exuberance in opposition to the curmudgeon Horace Vandergelder, and such overabundance always leads to bad things, regardless of how good the original thing is.

Vandergelder is, in the play, the richest merchant in Yonkers, a moderate city just north of New York City now, but in the early 1880s, the time period for this play, Yonkers had 20,000 people – for comparison, New York City had over one million by then. He runs a very successful general store. As such, he is ill-tempered about all things related to money. For example, when it comes time for the promotion of his chief clerk, Cornelius, Vandergelder promotes Cornelius to the position of chief clerk. When Cornelius asks for at least some extra benefits, Vandergelder fumes: "The world's going to pieces. You elegant ladies lie in bed until six and at nine o'clock at night you rush to close the door so fast the customers bark their noses. No, sir–you'll attend to the store as usual. . ."[1] Naturally, the merchant inspires a lot of rebellion. The most important such rebellion to the play is Dolly Levi's matchmaking of Ambrose, an artist, and Ermengarde, the merchant's niece. Vandergelder hates artists because they don't make money – even though Ambrose (allegedly) makes a good amount of money for himself and wants to support his lover with his artistry. Thus Vandergelder will not allow Ambrose and Ermengarde to meet at all, though they do out of his presence quite a lot anyway, let alone marry. Dolly's plan is rather simple:

"MRS. LEVI: 'I am taking Ermengarde to New York on the next train. I shall not take her to Miss Van Huysen's, as is planned [Vandergelder's attempt to put distance between the couple]; I shall take her to my house. . .'
"'At about six I shall take you both with me to the Harmonia Gardens Restaurant on the Battery. Mr. Vandergelder will be there and everything will be arranged.'"[2]

This plan works out, but not without many hijinks along the way. Dolly keeps herself pure in her motives because this is not something she intends to do completely without Vandergelder but rather something she intends to prevent him from stopping, and as such moves around him. She recognizes the lovers as pure, even though they are acting somewhat disreputable. In fact, she recognizes the nature of their seeming unacceptable actions is the direct effect of its purity given the opposition it is facing.

To stand against the world in this way is of course commanded, as the world will most certainly attempt to subvert those plans from the Lord which seek to undermine the world like how Vandergelder seeks to destroy his niece’s plans on account of not lining up with his own vision, but one must remember that rebellion is inherently sinful. It is more than just taking issue with the world, it is engaging the world on its own standards, something Christ was never remiss about. Not so pure in their attempts to subvert the merchant are Barnaby and Cornelius. They decide, with everyone else running off to New York City, that they should as well – only, they have Vandergelder's general store to run. They decide to abandon the general store, and as an excuse they ruin the store by blowing up some out of date canned tomatoes. "They'll make such a smell that customers can't come into the place for twenty-four hours. That'll get us an evening free."[3] Not only is this done under Vandergelder's nose, but also is done to spite anyone else who needed the general store that day, or the next. In New York City, they woo a girl who Vandergelder is pursuing, Irene, and Irene's friend and coworker Minnie, and ruin the relationship when Vandergelder finds them in Irene's shop. This is accidental, and Vandergelder's and Irene's relationship is quite ill-fated to begin with, but Cornelius and Barnaby are not faultless as the premise of their escape is what caused this, and divine Providence alone would allow such evil means to come to any good, though not without consequences. The runaways take the girls out to Harmonia for dinner, and pay by stealing a purse on the ground, which of course is Vandergelder's, with the help of one of the latter's new workers, Malachi. Malachi advises Cornelius on the matter,

"No, no–nurse one vice in your bosom. Give it the attention it deserves and let your virtues spring up modestly around it. Then you'll have the miser who's no liar. . . never support two weaknesses at the same time. It's your combination sinners–your lecherous liars and miserly drunkards–who dishonor the vices and bring them into bad repute."[4]

This view comes from the secular separation of different sins. It is the mindset that causes Barnaby and Cornelius to abandon their post in the first place. There is, of course, a physical distinction between lying and intoxication, between all sins, as they all have different effects on the world we live in, but in a philosophical and spiritual sense it is all rebellion against the Lord, and the degree of rebellion may differ but the type and severity never does. To lie is to steal is to kill, it is all directed against the Creator and seeks to harm Him and His Creation; one does not nurture one vice but nurtures sin, and sin cannot be nurtured in parts. This is ultimately what differs Dolly and the lover's undercutting of Vandergelder from Cornelius's and Barnaby's; Dolly is seeking to nurture what she in her wisdom can tell is a good thing, and is refusing to let anything stop a good thing from happening, whereas Cornelius and Barnaby are seeking to nurture their wicked sensibilities, their undying hunger for pleasure and the thrill of doing something bad. Truly it is not merely Vandergelder the latter is in opposition to but in opposition to Christ. They align themselves with the person they hate because they live their lives for the same purpose that the object of their hate does: unending stimulation and smug superiority:

"CORNELIUS: 'No. The best of all would be a person who has all the good things a poor person has, and all the good meals a rich person has, but that's never been known. No, you and I are going to be Vandergelders; all the more reason, then, for us to try and get some living and some adventure into us now–will you come, Barnaby?'"[5]

Cornelius will eventually feel guilt for stealing the purse and will give it to Dolly, as through Vandergelder's attempt to thwart Dolly's plan and a quite unbelievable set of coincidences caused by bad communication will result in the entire cast coming together at Van Huysen's house, who is with Dolly on her mission though not collaborating.

By the end of the play, everyone has found a place for themselves, mostly through Providence. With the assistance of Van Huysen and Dolly, Ambrose and Ermengarde come together. Cornelius and Irene, and Barnaby and Minnie, also couple together. At the last, Vandergelder and Dolly agree to marry. Dolly is the focus for this last moment. She comments to the audience, "Vandergelder's never tired of saying most of the people in the world are fools, and in a way he's right, isn't he? Himself, Irene, Cornelius, myself! But there comes a moment in everybody's life when he must decide if he'll live among human beings or not–a fool among fools or a fool alone."[6] Dolly is looking to remarry because she has stayed isolated for some years after her first husband's death. It is the shenanigans of the night in New York City and the way she was able to make things work out, the way that even the shenanigans of Barnaby and Cornelius managed to be worked out, the way that one can be lifted out of evil ambitions and find something truly pure, that convinces herself to take that kind of step and marry Vandergelder, someone she's known for a long time and someone she thinks she can do right by. Vandergelder himself is happy to marry. And, of course, as also is Dolly's plan all along, Vandergelder sets himself right:

"VANDERGELDER: 'A lot of foolishness. Everybody falling in love with everybody. I forgave 'em; Ermengarde and that artist.’"
MRS. LEVI: 'I knew your would.'
"VANDERGELDER: 'I made Cornelius Hackl my partner.'
"MRS. LEVI: 'You won’t regret it.' . . .
"VANDERGELDER: 'But you're a wonderful woman. Dolly, marry me.'"[7]

Christ came down not as a king but as a servant. He was libeled as a drunkard and glutton by those who followed something of the religion He established. He was by all accounts raffish, but good beyond just appearing cool or having some hazy positive affect on the world; it was Christ's raffishness, His want to be rebuked by those who would never come to know Him, that saved all of us, that has permitted us to join His inheritance, that defined Christ's sinless life. Many other religions see Christ's death on the cross as foolishness, unacceptable behavior for a god, they see Him stooping low as downright offensive, but this hatred of the Christ is their foolishness like Cornelius's and Barnaby's, seeking only to serve their rebellion, while the Christ is completely free in His pursuance of good things, much more free than we are now, but His seeming foolishness proves a strength we long for and will be granted. Let this be a reminder to all that Christ has explicitly chosen to work through us in spite of our sin; following Christ does not make us sinless, not yet. The Bible is the archetype for our lives, for it is the killers and destroyers and adulterers who build and lead Christ's Kingdom on Earth, and it is only the touch of the Lord that can turn their minds away from this and make them new beings. All of us collectively have hoped for a redemption, for a time when we will be unable to kill or ruin or fornicate, and Christ will make good on this promise and establish His Kingdom on Earth as it is in Heaven, and the desire in our hearts, which has now been made oriented towards Christ in our salvation, will be available to us in abundance.
