The Skin of our Teeth & Embodiment

Return to catalog

Everyone alive and dead at this moment was specifically created by Christ and is made in the image of Christ. The greatest evils of man cannot hide the beauty of God within themselves. An easy way to circumvent the obvious nature of the Triune God in life is to cover these aspects with a mental veneer and ascribe them to random and created deities, deities which typically only serve as a weak abstraction for Mankind. The one who has turned to Christ lives a life of harmony, for the body has aligned with that which it was created to do, but those who deny the Christ must attempt to live a life for Christ without Christ, as mankind cannot do that which it was not created to do. Thus we all attempt to find the divine in the flesh and assign godhood to the heathen. Each act of The Skin of Our Teeth takes real and venerable aspects of the human condition and refuses to identify them with their Creator. Godhood is not to be found in collective humanity [Genesis 11:1-9; Deuteronomy 1; 2 Chronicles 24:15-27] but divinity is found in the Lord alone [Exodus 15:1-11; Psalm 77; John 3; Revelation 15] and life can only be directed through and with Him. [1 Corinthians 6; 1 Peter 2:1-12]

Often godhood is assigned to humanity in the terms of community – in edt, "together we can do anything!" philosophies. In Act I, the Antrobus family and some notable human figures come together to weather out an ice age. The Antrobus family is the focus of the whole play. They are an everyfamily. They are a mother, father, son, daughter, and servant. The Antrobus family is quite dysfunctional. Maggie the mother is worldly and frantic, Henry the son is violent and emotional, Gladys the daughter is aloof and degraded, and Sabina the servant is antagonistic – she will go as far as skipping lines and disrupting the play to attempt to thwart the survival of the Antrobus family, though will always revert when she comes too close to her goal of annihilation. The play's Acts are each about surviving a different cataclysmic event. In Act I, it's an ice age. Many great thinkers enter the Antrobus house to try and warm out the ice age. George, the patriarch, has spent his time inventing the wheel and expanding the multiplication tables. He invites Homer, Moses, a Doctor, and others into the house because of their greatness. Henry's violence, Gladys's deviancy, Maggie's blindness and Sabina's aggression briefly demotivate George from his own survival, but the family attempt to build up his morale, even Sabina, for the fear of death overtakes them once George cannot continue. At the end of the Act, George pulls himself together and says "If you do come through this–what'll you be able to do? What do you know? Henry, did you take a good look at that wheel?"[1] The Antrobus family takes upon themselves the words and feelings of these great men and of themselves and deify them. Their focus is entirely on human endeavors, on the "human race," and how together they can do great things. The reason to keep going is because of making the wheel and discovering the physics and mathematics of how the world works. Ironically, Maggie's judgment of these things is accurate but only from her Christless position: "The earth's turning to ice and all he [George] can do is make up new numbers."[2] It is foremost Christ who grants these things, so to turn away from them is to turn towards the godless attitudes the family members always have. Of course, these things are the godless things you turn to if you reject the Lord. Furthermore, without Christ, survival is not worth anything. It is only the beauty of Christ that shows ultimate meaning in learning of His creations. It is also the mercy of Christ alone that perpetuates His most loved creation, human beings, for the Antrobus family only pulls through this dire moment in tatters and rags, a skeleton held together only by the gums in its skull. The human race, combined in this way, is nothing to Christ, and the behavior of the family alone shows this.

If one should directly acknowledge divinity in this framework, something beyond humanity, the nonbeliever only seeks vague and faint means for this. The Fortune Teller of Act II is a follower of such. She represents the divine, both in favor of the Antrobus family and working with Sabina; she promotes the antagonist but understands the necessity and inexorable nature of the hero's victory. The incoming calamity humanity must survive this act, that everyone except us and the Fortune Teller seem oblivious to, is a flood, likened to the same flood that wiped the evil off of the face of the Earth that the Lord sent in Noah's time. The Fortune Teller is the one to introduce the audience in this Act. She says directly to the audience "But first [before the flood] you'll see shameful things–shameful things. Some of you will be saying: 'Let him drown. He's not worth saving. Give the whole thing up.' I can see it in your faces. But you're wrong. Keep your doubts and despairs to yourselves." The Fortune Teller also remarks, "They're coming–the Antrobuses. Keck. Your hope. Your despair. Your selves."[3] She tells of the totality of the human experience – hope in Christ, despair without, everything in ourselves, but again has no real divinity, only the shadow of it, which she cannot ignore. Sabina attempts to tempt George, this time away from his wife and entirely to herself, but the calamity at hand binds everyone together again and George leads the animals and themselves onto a boat to wait out the flood. As the Fortune Teller prods the Antrobuses to leave and to live, she remarks, "George Antrobus! Think it over! A new world to make.–think it over!"[4] Divinity is unavoidable. The human race can be saved. Christ is the source of divinity and Christ is the source of salvation. These facts put most people in a dilemma: they must be honest with themselves and accept the divine nature of reality and seek to overcome, yet deny the Lordship of the Christ. They must make a good world, a new world, and yet are powerless to change reality themselves. The Fortune Teller can give the obvious right path and plain good news to people, and George and the family can survive, but she cannot save them herself. She can send the Antrobuses on a boat, but she cannot tell the boat where to land or give direction to them once they make it there. The people can follow their hearts, but they will always miss the mark. The Lord is particular and specific, and is not accepting of anyone or anything. Even if you turn to the cross, to the Bible, to God, to Church, to prayer, to service, if you do not turn to the person of the Lord, you will be lost, drifting, perhaps mercifully safe from the storm in your lifeboat but a castaway nonetheless, let alone how lost you will be if you turn to ungrounded mystics. The Antrobuses survive as a people who fornicate away from their wives and kill any who wrong them in any way and scandal their selves, not even for the appeal of other men but just for themselves, as George and Henry and Gladys all respectively do throughout the play. That is not salvation. That is mercy, mercy that will be shut off one day. This is not despair, for through Christ we will see through these things as the Fortune Teller suggests, but Christ is not some vague religious experience or feeling of sonder. Consider also, the Second Act has George president of Order of the Mammals, to represent the superiority of the humankind over the rest of the natural world. He can feel his Imagio Dei but cannot express it on account of being separated from the Lord. The divinity of humankind is obvious but must be presented as rationality or logic, and often can only be honestly credited to randomness, as George remarks "I do not deny a few months before my birth I hesitated between. . . uh. . . between pinfeathers and gill-breathing–and so did many of us here,–but for the last million years I have been viviparous, hairy, and diaphragmatic."[5] Among other things, a remark on man's lack of agency on his own.

Most of all, then, without Christ the human life has no real direction. In Act III of the play, the perspective is shifted somewhat, as now we are shown after a catastrophe has happened, this time a war, likened to the Second World War. It is also the play itself that is struggling to survive now; a lot of actors have been lost to food poisoning during the intermission. Nonetheless the play goes on. Henry is more explicitly shown as evil in this act, as he comes home from the war hating his father, shown as unambiguously evil for valuing his own freedom over the lives of the rest of the human race. His evilness is only so ascribed to him because his ambitions become more explicitly self-centered – instead of violence with the human race, he wants violence in his isolation, and to the playwright to turn away from society is the worse thing one can do, as opposed to turning away from Christ. The play begins to fall apart – George and Henry cannot even say their lines and have to pause for a moment because the scene is too close to home for Henry. This dishevels George. He sits down and says:

"I've lost it. This minute I feel like all those people dancing around the bonfire – just relief. Just the desire to settle down; to slip into the old grooves and keep the neighbors from walking over my lawn. – Hm. But during the war, – in the middle of all that blood and dirt and hot and cold – every day and night, I'd have moments, Maggie, when I saw the things we could do when it was over. When you're at war you think about a better life; when you're at peace you think about a more comfortable one. I've lost it. I feel sick and tired."[6]

George picks himself back up and lists his gods: "Now I remember what three things always went together when I was able to see things more clearly: three things. Three things: . . . The voice of the people and their confusion and their need. And the thought of you and the children and this house. . . and. . . my books!"[7] At the end, of course, George returns to his heroes, philosophers like Spinoza and Aristotle, and Moses in the form of the beginning of Genesis, and he returns to this mentality of wanting to move forward, to be better, as though these things mean anything, as though it is not the blind fumbling of human willpower attempting to subvert the restorative power of Christ and the wisdom of the Lord that even causes earthquakes and ice ages and floods and wars. Henry is wrong for his views, but the attempt to subvert those views with vague collectivism and uncertain momentum falls almost short. For us, it will fall short entirely one day. One day Christ will no longer suspend us over the pit of Hell but will allow us to fall in face-first, and the likes of George will progress into the eternal damnation gleefully. The play begins as it ended, with Sabina cleaning the house and the family separated, which is poignant. It does not need to be said that unfaithfulness, ultraviolence, sexual debauchery, and amoralness will persist and perpetuate on loop, in repeated iterations, for all eternity aside from Christ. This is the goal of the godless. Those who turn away from Christ seek these things explicitly. Philosophers and servitude will not fill the whole in your heart that longs for Christ. Sabina's rallying cry at the end of the play, "This is where you came in. We have to go on for ages and ages yet,"[8] to the Christian is a dreadful threat. How awful that the world would play out its loops against the Lord, knowing full well both the consequences in the end and the horror of the journey itself. How debased the human race is to take joy in these things.

None of this implies that a life directed towards Christ will be easier than the life as shown in the play, that the obstacles in one's life will be magically done away with. Christ does not operate on some extrareality layer but within His own Creation which He created for this express purpose. Looking towards Christ will not immediately elevate you out of this world and into some other world. Simply, looking towards Christ is the right thing to do and looking away from Christ is the wrong thing to do. Christ is merciful to all of us to let us live in any capacity as the sinful beings we are, but He does not do this merely because He can or because He is being nice, but for the expressed purpose of our salvation. [2 Peter 3:1-10] Consider that the Lord would suspend the Earth from annihilation simply to be able to love you, and so you could love Him also. That Christ died so that you would find your proper place and submit everything to Him and his Law and die to your sin. There will come a day when you don't make it out. You are not granted this survival the Antrobuses have enjoyed. You may not even have another seven years. When Christ asked the man to follow Him who wanted to tend to his dead family, Christ was not accommodating to the man's hesitancy – for the man did not say "I will follow you Christ, but what of my father's funeral?" but instead turned his mind away from Christ in the moment – Christ rather was sorry He had asked the man to join Him in the first place. Christ may be sorry to have looked upon you, too. Thus you must come to Him and draw near to Him.
