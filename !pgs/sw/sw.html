Salad Wizard & Trust

Return to catalog

At her outset, Salad Wizard is an enigma, purposefully keeping herself as much of a secret from the world as she can. Nonetheless, it is obvious she is a sorceress. As she travels, she picks up other young and admiring sorcerers, an innkeeper girl named Flara Laura Serapion and a vagabond named Solse, both homeless outcasts. The pair become more frightened of Salad Wizard the closer they grow to her, but they pledge to remain loyal. Serapion tells her, "I'm sorry for saying there's no point for you to survive. You won&aps;t abuse your power. You wouldn't go to war against the world. . . if there wasn't something wrong with it. I'll fight beside you, mistress. . ."[1] Her affection and loyalty seem so strong, so bold. She’s conspiring with the other apprentice to halt Salad Wizard's plans twelve pages later. Salad Wizard is gracious to them, and they come around to trusting her finally at the end. Human beings can be so fickle. The world does not make this easy; who is there really to trust, anyway? There is Jesus Christ, of course, but we need human connection too. What are we to do? We are to trust anyway, with fear and trembling. The characters in Salad Wizard have a hard time understanding Salad Wizard's motives but know she is a good person and a role model and continue to follow her; we would do good both to remember that we must extend these graces to the people in our lives and know how much grace the Lord extends to us.

Serapion is very interested in Salad Wizard based just on her usage of magic. Serapion is embedded with strong magic, but in her past she has been hated for it – it only materializes as some kind of magic explosion when she's upset. Serapion used this to blow up her mother's wedding because of how her mother hated her. This is not control, of course, but prediction. Serapion wants to use her magic for good, and she knows Salad Wizard uses her magic for good, so she attaches to the latter to the annoyance of the sorceress. For Salad Wizard's part, she simply believes to be Serapion to be a mindless idolizer and attempts to cast the kid off of her because of this, but her interest is birthed when Serapion, of course quite upset by the attempted ditch, blows up and proves herself capable of wielding magic. This occurrence is also Serapion's first negative experience with Salad Wizard: "I trusted you! I thought you wouldn't treat me like a kid!"[2] After this exchange, the relationship between the pair is deepened, for Salad Wizard knows Serapion to be more than just a fan of Salad Wizard, and Serapion knows Salad Wizard will take care of her, even if it ends up not being the specific way Serapion wishes – though Salad Wizard does personally take on Serapion.

This starts the trust-untrust cycle as Serapion struggles with knowing Salad Wizard's goodness firsthand and yet not being able to reconcile Salad Wizard's actions entirely with the sorceress's goodness. As the pair travel together, Serapion discovers more about who Salad Wizard is and what the might sorceress wants to do – gather as many weapons of war as she can. They do a town they cross a favor and pick up Solce, a descendant of a sorcerer and able to use magic to create light. Serapion hates this at first too but quickly comes around. As they begin to know Salad Wizard more deeply, they become somewhat disillusioned for her vision. Salad Wizard's first stop is to examine and fight a plant monster she knows about as a test for her own plant monster. Their second stop is to explore the ruins of the infamous Sorcerer's Guild and pick up a powerful bomb the Guild had created. The children hate both events. They cannot connect all of the moving parts the way an uninvolved reader can, but even so the reader is also left out of Salad Wizard's mysteries. All we are really given of her directly is her small exposition when the kids are scared to see the sorceress's monster: "I used to love. . . creating plants that would bloom beautifully. I didn't know my purpose. I thought my magic was weak and useless. Then. . . I lost everything. I swore I wouldn't let that happen again. . . No one would respect a sorcerer who just treats plants."[3] In Separion's town, Salad Wizard is specifically to help an important tree grow. This seems to be an exception to how magic users are viewed in this world though; most people fear the mention of the Sorcerer's Guild and associate magic directly with them, and Serapion's parents abandoned her specifically because they thought her magic was cursed. So, Salad Wizard is trying to adopt this view the world has pushed on her. Salad Wizard wants to have a meaningful and impactful life, and that's why she's searching for all of these weapons, so she can be strong. Regretfully, she seems to view her "weaker" parts, those that would not have directly contributed to saving the Sorcerer's Guild, as failures. As mentioned before, it is at the point of the that the disciples, especially Serapion, grow close to Salad Wizard. You can even see Serapion grow close enough to look like Salad Wizard does for a moment when she is apologizing. Even so, Solce and Serapion seek to betray Salad Wizard anyway. It would not be correct to suppose that either belief the disciples have – their detesting and their admiration – are false. The truth is that the disciples love and admire Salad Wizard and understand that she has a plan for good and not for evil, but their child minds are unable to reconcile Salad Wizard's view for a better world with her collection of such violent instruments. Of course, there is no real need to reconcile these two things, but nonetheless the pair ultimately cannot trust Salad Wizard, "I'm afraid it's clear now. Mistress is dangerous,"[4] and decide to steal away with the bomb and the plant monster.

Of course, Salad Wizard cannot really be thwarted. In fact, the disciples willingly go right back to her without meaning to, because she already knows their plans and how to stop them. She has no trouble whatsoever taking the bomb back from them. She doesn't even have to use much magic, just one "punishment arrow" to trip the plant monster, catch the pair in the same plant the arrow summons, and retrieve the box with the bomb all at once. The kids cannot understand Salad Wizard at all and rely on their own understanding to guide them. Of course, Salad Wizard protects herself through being an enigma, but they simply let their carnal intuition blind what is already obvious to them and confessed by them. Separion says, "Of course your cute disciples would say bye-bye to you if you kept obsessing over these things, duh!! You love a bomb more than us! What even is that!? This isn't right! This isn't right! This can't be your purpose, mistress! Please, reconsider!"[5] Separion knows within her that Salad Wizard has her best interests at heart but lets her surface level judgments warp her vision. She understands the world has put Salad Wizard down and disrespects her kind, as Separion's own parents hated her for her own magic, but still cannot see Salad Wizard's search for a place in a broken world. She cannot trust Salad Wizard to be a good person, to make the right choice, and of course such things can never be scientifically verified, so she untrusts. She tries to take matters into her own hands. This kind of thing never works out. Even if Salad Wizard did not stop the pair, they would have been actively making the situation worse anyway.

Salad Wizard, seeking not to upset her disciples but needing to go forth with her plan, disposes of the bomb but keeps the component within it that she was after to begin with, the bomb's core. Salad Wizard is gracious to her disciples. She does not owe them any remorse or mercy. She has a plan and she has the authority to stick to it. By all rights, she should have abandoned her disciples in the old guildhouse and taken the bomb and continued on her plan, free of their burden, but she doesn't do this. She tends to her disciples' worries and sticks by them in a way that doesn't undermine her own authority nor derail her plans. For the sorceress's part, her thoughts about Serapion during this ordeal are stated explicitly: "Good grief, ranting as always. . . but I see you're not exploding. You've learned to control yourself a little, eh? Good progress, disciple."[6] The pair leaving Salad Wizard's good graces is never even a thought in the sorceress's mind. They have before this point permanently become her disciples, and Salad Wizard makes effort to keep them. Despite the annoyance they bring her, they help her too. We can see, at this final confrontation, Salad Wizard enjoying the smell of a little flower, something she had felt embarrassed by and felt worthless. We see that Salad Wizard does value these children because their hope for a world that is not blighted rekindles the sorceress's own flame for such a world. It returns her to her love of cultivation. She drops her persona and becomes herself, connects with the kids. Then, the sorceress goes beyond even this: "We should get going. You must be tired. Let's go back to town and get a good rest."[7] She gives them treasure they passed over later to take with them for good use. She extends a lot of grace towards her disciples because she wants them to be satisfied. She loves them. The disciples reward her with renewed affection, and hopefully a better commitment to her as well.

Trusting in other people is necessary to live a life that honors Christ, and it will always hurt you. There is no person now living on the face of the planet who will never hurt you. No matter how much someone loves you or adores you, they are incapable of loving you perfectly on this Earth. You nonetheless need the imperfect love of your brothers and sisters. Take hope and comfort in that the Lord is faithful and trustworthy and that we all strive to be as He is. [Deuteronomy 7; 2 Kings 19; Psalm 31; 1 Corinthians 1:1-9; Philippians 3] Take hope that one day we will be as He is in this manner. It is for this reason that we follow after Christ, that we may know true fellowship with another being, not only mere humans but the one who spoke us into existence. We should be eternally grateful to have such a longwithstanding Lord.
