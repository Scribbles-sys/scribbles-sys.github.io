She Doesn't Know Why She Lives Volume 2 & Progress

Return to catalog

How do we live in a sinful world? We absolutely cannot give in to the sinful tendencies of this world. We cannot let our labor become fruitless, our connections dead, and our life experience meaningless. We must fight against these things, but the world we live in is entirely devoted to them, and Christ in His wisdom has left us in this world. Kanade ends the first volume by saying, "It feels like a new path has opened up for me. When I get home, I'm going to take a step back and reflect."[1] So, what does Kanade do? What does Izumi do to help her life out? The truth, really, is nothing. There is nothing one can do. One can only focus on personal salvation, on growing more righteous in our hearts, can cope with the life they have been given. We have no hope of improving life because we have no hope to fight against sin, and Christ’s will is that we live in this sinful world for a time. So, we must focus on moving forward through life. This is progress in the personal state. This is what Christ calls us to do. We are to keep going. We are to draw nearer to Him. [Micah 6:1-8; Habakkuk 3; John 16:25-33; Romans 12; 2 Timothy 2:22-26, 3:1-9]

Izumi and Kanade manage to find fulfillment in the work she does. Even if their work is not what they want, contributing their best is what will keep them through their job, focusing on what they can do as opposed to what they can’t, not drifting endlessly between unfulfilling careers or sulking in the one they have. Izumi knows this from personal experience; before she helped run an amusement center, she was an office worker. Izumi starts this book reflective of her life. Of her job, she says a lot.

"It's only been a years since I joined my company. At first, I thought I'd get used to the job eventually. . .I'm not exactly capable enough to get all my work done by then in the first place. It kind of hit me after I started working, but I wasn't all that much better than the others at the job. . .Why am I treading down this path that I know is full of pain and nothing else? . . . I want to quit my job. . ."[2]

There is, again, a lot one can say and not say about office jobs, but regardless Izumi is reflecting here on feelings that never really went away. She still feels empty and incompetent tuning arcade machines and stocking prizes. These feelings never go away, but rather are remedied by reality. "I don't think she's [Izumi's] aware of it, but she's really good at tuning the machines. She was really meticulous about it, too," Kanade says to their manager. "I'll do what I can to support her, so she can continue to be even more meticulous while she works!"[3] Izumi only catches the last, but it soothes and quells her doubts. She can hear and see some impacts her work is having on others. In spite of everything else that is wrong, people do come to the arcade, people do have fun, and Izumi is responsible for that and participates in that. Izumi knows she cannot expect the way she feels to fade nor who she has fundamentally been created as to change, but she comes to realize that she is not preordained for despair, at the least.

Most importantly, Izumi manages to want a friend. Kanade inserts herself into Izumi's life, and Izumi does not merely allow her to exist with her but progresses to enjoying her presence and sharing her life with her. As Izumi puts it, "At first, I was completely frustrated at my situation, but Kanade-chan kept acting energetic as usual. Talking to her when she's like that just made me feel dumb for being such an awful person. And recently, the reality that Kanade-chan was going to be gone soon was starting to dawn on me. . ."[4] Though at this point she has yet to really allow Kanade to be her friend, but she is coming around to opening up – though she would certainly be the last person she knows to adopt the term. In fact, Izumi comes to really care and fear over her and Kanade's friendship. It is a beautiful thing. Of course, Kanade seeks after this as well. As much as Kanade has helped Izumi, Izumi has helped Kanade; as much as Izumi has feared over Kanade, Kanade has feared over Izumi: "I was incredibly scared. Scared of not being able to do things right at my new place, and at my new job. But with this notebook, I think Ill be able to stay optimistic. It's all thanks to the two of you [Izumi and the arcade manager] for your support."[5] Instead of chasing what was immediately in front of her, and sitting around in a purely reflective state, as Izumi is prone to do Kanade commits to a friendship and allows Izumi to see that friendships are more fulfilling than the peace of loneliness, that action can be demanding but is fulfilling and necessary. Kanade's life doesn't improve per se – such a task is so monumental that perhaps not even the whole of society could come together and make life as a concept better – but she is able to feel better and do better at work; while Kanade has other friends, she is able to connect to Izumi in a way that she isn't able to with the other people she hangs out with. It is by doing this that she is able to share with Izumi the joys of these things, the "way to deal with your issue" the arcade manager mentioned to the two of them at the end of the last volume.

It is through this that Izumi manages to find meaning in her life. For example, Izumi has an issue with watching a lot of television. Usually, everything she watches brings her down. She watches a kid who's professional at table tennis, a sport Izumi was committed to in middle school, and despairs over the lack of such things in her own life. Instead of just sulking like usual, though, she acts. "I'm turning 26 next year. . . What am I even thinking? I'm already painfully aware that it's too late to be drowning in regrets now. I really need to stop my thoughts from always being affected by this mindset. Alright. . . for now, watching this isn't doing me any favors. Let's watch something else."[6] The next program she watches is of a marathon runner who's older than her. Izumi tries to work out like she is, and despairs because she is in so much worse shape than in middle school, but she has both came out better for her efforts and correctly oriented her efforts. It would be rude to expect Izumi to have healed now, silly to have expected her to be perfected by now. All Izumi can do is look in the right direction and go there. The orientation of the mind is the most important thing for progress; you will go where you are looking. Again, Izumi tries to put herself and the progress she is making down, "All I did was listen though, it's not like I did anything. . . If anything, it was the manager who. . ." but Kanade confirms for Izumi, because they both need it: "Of course, I'm very grateful to the manager, too. But I'm here today because you went along with me back then."[7] It is this that gives Izumi's life forward motion: joy and care for her labor, a friend she can commit to, and a life she can envelop herself in. It is great care in tuning arcade cranes and efficiency in filling out paperwork, it is courage to reach out to friends for help with mundane life tasks like growing flowers, and it is the vision of participating with television programs and in climbing the steps to the problems of your life.

In thinking on Kanade's gradation from their workplace to a bigger store, Izumi becomes quite jealous and sad, and sulks on how much better Kanade is than her and how much easier Kanade's life seems to be. It is more wondrous, then, that Izumi should be so attached as to genuinely miss Kanade's presence by the end of the volume. It is because Izumi has settled, has turned herself. We cannot expect gee-whiz rockets, infinite resources of asteroid mining, freedom from oppression secular or religious, people to understand our plight, mechanisms that never break down on us, we cannot expect anything. We live as in a false dawn. The Light and where it is coming from is clear and bright and obvious but provides no illumination itself over this world. That Final Dawn has not arrived yet. Rather, such Light shows itself to us here as in a thunderstorm in that fake dawn. It is wrathful, grieved beyond grief at this dark and empty world. It is a light that shines out everything else, blurring outlines until all that is left is blank white, pure rage. When the Lord cries out, He is not merely in rage but in pain. He is hurt because we hurt Him, this world has hurt Him, as put Him on a cross and given Him all of its own evil. Our pain pains Him. The Lord does not sit and sulk. The Lord is in every place and at every moment both working for His Will and for the ultimate salvation of His followers and resting in the goodness of these things. The Lord will impart judgment on this world, and He will not bring Himself to be nice about it. Until that day of freedom, we must cope with the darkness, and as the fitful thunder rages, the light of the lightning guides the path of the believers. We turn to Him because there is nothing else to turn to. We praise Him because there is no one else to praise.
