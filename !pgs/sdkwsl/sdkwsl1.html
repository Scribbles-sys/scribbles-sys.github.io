Izumi lives an awful life. She opens She Doesn't Know Why She Lives in a bout of despair, for as the title suggests, her life has seemed to hit an end. There doesn't seem to be anything left. Izumi is a person who is responsible for herself and her attitude and her actions, but Izumi also inhabits a space, place, a culture, a land, a world. Izumi mulls over the three basic problems with modern life: “Why am I alive? It's not because I want to work. It's not so I can talk to people, either. And I don't think it's so I can eat."[1] How sad that Izumi dismisses out of hand the three things we really are made to do: to labor and work the Earth, [Genesis 2:15] to connect with other people, [Genesis 2:18] and to be enriched – not in the sense of simply feeling good, but of being completely enveloped in living life – not to eat something just because it tastes good but to be immersed in the experience of well prepared food and having your own life be sustained, for example. [Genesis 1:28-31; 2:25] We have not merely lost these things; every person who has come before us has labored without rest nor end to be unshackled by the Lord's will for life. We have woken up in a world without these things.

Perhaps one of the most controversial subjects of the modern era is of labor and working. What does it mean to work? Christ has created the world to be labored. When the Lord put man in his preordained place, the Garden of Eden, the paradise we hope for, it wasn't "to increase his ability to praise," for this ability to praise and give thanks is innate to man, nor was it "to do good works for the Lord," for the ability to do good things is innate to the Lord, but "to cultivate it and keep it," as the NASB95 puts it; "to work it and keep it," as the ESV puts it. [Genesis 2:15] Knowing labor in this way, it is not so surprising that it would be so ruined in the world that we live in, nor that there would be so much discussion on when and why and how to do labor. Certainly working the garden would not have involved things like sending emails endlessly at an office computer, nor calling people all day to buy insurance, nor offhandedly patch some mechanical or software issue that deserves more intensive care. Nonetheless Izumi finds herself manning a family fun center for a living. There is a lot one could say and one could suppress when it comes to such places, but certainly it is obvious that Paradise was not creates for one to merely take money from people and put knick-knacks in claw machines, no matter how cute the knick-knacks are. Izumi's worker Kanade tells her so much when they hang out after work one day: "I've yet to find a job that I want to do, but it's not like my current lifestyle is causing me any problems. So if I were to continue like that with no change, in the next year, in the next five years, and ten years, I'm sure the one waiting will be me, but without any of the good parts that make me today – it'll just be me in a state of recline."[2] Of course, the issue is just as much as not being satisfied with good labor as it is with abolishing said labor, but this fact only further shows the ubiquity of fallen labor. The labor has been distorted beyond all words, so that no work is meaningful or fulfilling or beneficial or contributive or any such thing. Working it doesn't impact Izumi at all; she can't even be allowed to feel like she's acting on society in a negative way. She isn't acting at all. Sin has left her disconnected.

Similarly controversial, though more recent, is the problem of connection. What does it mean to be connected? Christ has created us to be in eternal communion with Him, and in eternal communion with Him we become enjoined as one body, one Church, connected in the deepest way possible, through our Creator. There is no other way to connect. So, to thwart this, the world has adopted the antitheticals of all of these: short relationships, focus on divisive factors, killing all genuine emotions, and focusing on comfort and self-peace. We all turn away from difficult conversations, from inconvenient favors, from committing to any of our friends in any serious way, from connecting with that which we have together, from understanding what we don't have together, from exploring the psyche and tracing the paths of our close ones. Truly, Izumi disregards all of this. When a coworker asks her to hang out, she cowers and feels cornered. She only ever does things because she feels like she has to, whether those things are running away from someone or hanging out with them. When Kanade calls waiting to see Izumi at work after the latter is sick – because Kanade feels she can only connect with Izumi in certain ways, as the audience will find out, Izumi can only think "I wish she hadn't called and just left me alone. When someone urges you to do something, their feelings start to weigh on you, to the point that it drives you out of obligation, even more so when you're by yourself."[3] Izumi has no friends. Izumi cannot even bring herself to want to talk to her coworkers and dodges people as much as she can.

"There are several supermarkets in this area, so if I had to choose, I would settle for the closest one. . . But it also happens to be in the building I work in. I don't know why, but it's always times like this when you go out that you bump into people you know. . . God, I do not want that. Annoying, but I'll go to the other supermarket [an extra 15 minutes walk] this time."[4]

How awful to want to avoid the few people on this world you will be granted the grace of being intimate with. Why is Izumi like this? Simply because it seems a waste. When your work seems hollow, your hobbies seem ruined, and your enjoyment of life has been systematically destroyed, doing anything seems a waste. Connection between other people has been ruined. This does not change the fact that human beings are created to and commanded to connect. Connection is the culmination of this volume, as that is what Kanade's and Izumi's time hanging out together at the end is about. Kanade has found herself in the same mindset as Izumi and she desperately wants out, so she reaches out to the only person she knows that she can, which is Izumi. The talk between them is quite despairing and yet, as the quote before proves, fulfilling. "We neutralized our anxieties by sharing them with each other. That alone was enough to put my current self at ease."[5]

Something everyone seems to focus their entire lives around is an enriching existence. Though it is easy to dismiss this as an attempt to serve both one's self and Christ, the truth is that Christ does seek satisfaction for us. What Christ does not seek, and what man is chiefly concerned with on his own, is φιλήδονος (philedonos,) which is to be obsessed with good feelings. This word is only used once in the New Testament to describe people who's fondness of the world and sin far exceeds their love and care for the Lord. [2 Timothy 3:4] One of the base words, ἡδονή (hedone,) is used other times and to similar affect. [Luke 8:14; Titus 3:3; James 4:1-3; 2 Peter 2:13] Izumi sees this now: at work, people come together to play games and not much else. When she eats, she eats alone and only so she can sustain the life she has begun to hate. When she walks, it's only so she can be somewhere. When she talks to someone, she can only think of how it can benefit her, and if it can't benefit her she can only think of how she can run away from the conversation so she can feel good. The internal love for these things, the quality the Lord has given them, has been obscured beyond discovery.

"I've been. . . completely left behind by them [her friends], haven't I? Well, duh. I've made zero progress in my life. I'm stuck in place, forced against the wall of time. But how am I supposed to proceed if I can't see where I'm going? I don't have anything that could light my way, like the headlights of a car. How does everyone manage to do it when they don't even know what direction to take? The path used to be so bright for me back then, but I can't go back that way anymore."[6]

Loving things can be so difficult. Difficulty does not absolve one of responsibility. Responsibility does not relieve one of pain. Pain does not justify doing the wrong thing. It is a vicious system, as vicious as it's father, Mankind. Christ created us not to feel good but to be good. We do not take any pleasure in being with someone who has had a parent or child or spouse die, but we be with them because the experience is enriching for both people. When we stay with a pet until it dies, we are heartbroken but we feel fulfilled. When we help a lazy friend try to clean their house or find a job, we feel frustrated but we feel an ecstasy of progressing, too. It is fulfilling because it is what we were meant to do.

When you take away fulfillment, take away bonds with others, and take away proper work, life becomes a waste. This wasted world has been the end goal, the ultimatum, the dream of every single person on the Earth since the Fall. People like to joke about killing Victorian London kids by giving them the candies we eat today, but perhaps the kid would only die of ecstasy. Finally, finally, the past two thousand years of systematically denying the Lord of Jacob and Isaac and Israel, of crucifying the Christ and extinguishing the Holy Spirit over and over and over, finally humanity would have made it. Finally we would have shrugged off the burden of a loving and caring Christ who watches over us and awakened within ourselves our own deities. Finally, no more shared mealtime with family or friends or healthy relationships with other people or vulnerability or caring for humanity or purpose or drive or cogency or any other such nonsense. The Victorian child would die happy knowing the end goal of themselves and all of humanity would one day be realized. Perhaps the Victorian child would be able to die happy knowing one day the world's rejection of Christ would be complete and final. This is the world you and I and Izumi have woken up to. But then, it seems we don't really want that, do we? When we express anger at the innocence of youth or push people away who inconvenience us, we know inherently something is not right about that, even if it is what we want. When we eat junk food in between our meals and live every moment stuffing ourselves with some kind of media, we understand there's something much better than this, better for us and for everyone else as well. When we push off our friends, we understand that we are truly meant to be with them instead. We understand that there is a Christ, and as such we are damned eternally and maximally when we turn out backs from Him.
